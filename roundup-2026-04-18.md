1. **America Let China Win the Open-Source AI Race — Now Policymakers Are Trying to Reverse the Damage**
Coverage: Wall Street Journal

The real story isn't about open-source philosophy. It's about market share: 80% of developers worldwide who use open-source AI tools are now building with Chinese models, according to Andreessen Horowitz general partner Martin Casado. Chinese open models reached 30% of all AI usage in peak weeks last year. Alibaba's Qwen family crossed 700 million downloads in January to become the most widely adopted open-source AI system on the planet.

This didn't happen because China out-engineered the U.S. It happened because American policymakers spent two crucial years treating open-source AI as a threat. Proposed legislation, regulatory uncertainty, and the specter of compliance requirements — including California's SB 1047, which would have required open-source developers to monitor and control all downstream uses of their models — pushed American developers toward proprietary systems. Chinese builders faced no such headwinds and filled the vacuum.

**The policy shift:** The NTIA and BIS have both concluded the risks of open models don't justify restrictions. There is now bipartisan consensus in support of open-source AI. But the authors — AI policy leads at Andreessen Horowitz — argue consensus alone won't close the gap. Two concrete moves are required: federal agencies should default to open-source licenses when building AI tools with taxpayer dollars, and government procurement should stop quietly favoring proprietary vendors through requirements that ignore total cost of ownership and vendor lock-in.

**The competitive signal:** If 80% of open-source developers are building on Chinese models, then Chinese design choices and data governance assumptions are being embedded into the global developer ecosystem. That's not just a policy risk — it's a supply chain risk for every organization that depends on where open-source AI innovation happens next.

RMS Relevance: If 80% of open-source developers are building on Chinese model infrastructure, then vulnerabilities, design choices, and potential backdoors in those models propagate into applications globally — including tools that can find their way into defense workflows. For RMS programs, any dependency on software built on Chinese model foundations is a mission assurance question, not just a procurement preference. This connects directly to risk language LM has already flagged publicly about AI-enabled adversaries — and it means the open-source ecosystem's tilt toward China is worth tracking not as a policy story, but as a supply chain security signal for every program team evaluating third-party AI tools. Worth raising explicitly in program-level AI tool evaluation processes where provenance and model lineage matter.

2. **$1.8 Billion, Two People, $20,000: The Gap Between Chatting With AI and Working Through It**
Coverage: New York Times

Matthew Gallagher didn't use AI to make himself more efficient. He used it to make himself sufficient — across engineering, marketing, customer service, and business analytics simultaneously. That's a different mode of working than most people mean when they say they "use AI."

When Gallagher launched Medvi in September 2024, he wasn't asking a chatbot to review his emails. He used Claude, ChatGPT, and Grok to write production code for his software platform. He built custom AI agents — autonomous systems that operate without prompting — to connect his software platforms, route customer inquiries, and surface business performance data. He deployed ElevenLabs voice AI to communicate with customers directly. Midjourney and Runway generated all his ad creative. The startup cost: $20,000 and two months.

In 2025 — Medvi's first full year — the company generated $401 million in revenue and $65 million in net profit, a 16.2% margin. His only hire was his brother Elliot. This year Medvi is on track for $1.8 billion in revenue.

**The leverage ratio is what makes this concrete.** Hims & Hers competes in the same GLP-1 telehealth market with 2,442 employees, $2.4 billion in revenue, and a 5.5% net profit margin. Gallagher runs at comparable revenue scale with two people and a margin nearly three times higher. That isn't productivity improvement — it's a different operating model entirely. AI isn't assisting the functions that would normally require departments. It's running them.

**The human augmentation pattern is what makes this replicable.** Gallagher isn't an AI researcher. He's a marketer who knows how to acquire customers in a regulated market and learned which tools to point at which problems. Domain expertise plus AI orchestration fluency — that's the combination. The seven account managers Medvi eventually hired each handle hundreds of client relationships using AI to do what would otherwise require a much larger team. It's not AI instead of humans. It's humans operating at a leverage ratio that wasn't previously available.

RMS Relevance: The Hims vs. Medvi comparison isn't really a story about headcount reduction — it's a story about capacity at a fixed workforce size. For RMS, that distinction is critical right now. Lockheed Martin was in the White House meeting where defense contractors agreed to accelerate output — with Trump calling for quadrupling production of critical weapons systems. That demand signal arrives faster than the defense industrial base can hire for. And it hits especially hard on fixed-price programs — roughly 60% of LM's revenue — where you're committed to deliver at a set cost regardless of how much the surrounding analytical work grows. AI-augmented operating models address both pressures simultaneously: every hour AI handles on EVM aggregation, schedule variance analysis, or risk documentation is an hour a PM or engineer can spend on the work that actually moves production forward, without adding cost to a fixed-price commitment. The leverage the Medvi story demonstrates isn't about needing fewer people. It's about existing people being able to own more, at the same cost structure, without dropping quality or compliance. In an environment where the demand signal is accelerating, cleared workforce pipelines are finite, and contracts don't flex with scope, that's not an efficiency story. It's a delivery story.

3. **AI's Reliability Gap Is Closing — and the Engineering Stack That Got It There Is Now Visible**
Coverage: Wall Street Journal

For most of generative AI's short public history, the core problem was reliability: models that hallucinated confidently, invented facts, and failed at basic math. The question was never whether AI was impressive. It was whether it was trustworthy enough to do real work. A recent unintentional leak of Anthropic's Claude Code source code — combined with public disclosures from OpenAI and Google — illuminates how that changed.

**Better knowledge, better retrieval:** Early models were trained on static datasets and then frozen. Now models are continuously augmented with purpose-built training data from paid human domain experts, and given real-time web retrieval to fill gaps. OpenAI reports its current main model issues 26% fewer factual errors than GPT-4o two years ago. Anthropic says Claude's distinguishing characteristic in independent evaluations is "calibration" — knowing what it doesn't know, and saying so.

**Tool use, not just language generation:** The fundamental reliability problem with LLMs is that they were guessing at answers rather than computing them. Now models recognize when a question requires calculation and hand it off to traditional software: calculators, code execution, structured search. The Claude Code leak revealed this architecture explicitly — a layered system combining LLM output with conventional programming logic, including a memory management module to reduce context overload and a script that detects user frustration. As AI researcher Gary Marcus put it: "LLMs themselves are more or less just as unreliable as they were ever. But in places like math and coding, you can pass off the output of LLMs to other technologies that actually are very useful."

**Models checking each other:** Chain-of-thought reasoning — where an AI works through a problem before answering — has reduced error rates meaningfully. But the frontier approach goes further. Some production deployments now use what practitioners call a "council of models": Claude checking ChatGPT's output, an answer accepted only if multiple models agree. The result is lower error rates on high-stakes outputs without requiring model quality to improve.

RMS Relevance: These three mechanisms — knowledge injection, tool use, and multi-model verification — are the design principles behind the AI Factory's architecture, and they're worth understanding explicitly as we evaluate AI for higher-stakes program workflows. The 26% error reduction is a concrete calibration point for anyone asking whether AI is ready for EVM analysis or risk identification. The "council of models" approach maps directly to how we should design AI-assisted outputs in regulated or consequential program contexts — not trusting a single model, but building verification into the workflow. As Genesis and AI Factory capabilities mature, this reliability architecture is the technical grounding for where and how we extend AI into more critical program functions.
