# AI Strategy Library for Executives

## A Practitioner's Reference for Navigating the AI Transition

---

## What this library is

This library distills business and technology strategy lessons from six HBS cases spanning the full landscape of enterprise AI:

| # | Case | Company / Topic | Core Strategic Question |
|---|------|----------------|------------------------|
| 01 | Generative AI Value Chain | Industry-level analysis | Where does value sit in the AI stack, and where is it moving? |
| 02 | Moderna: Democratizing AI | Moderna | How do you scale AI across an enterprise without losing control? |
| 03 | Salesforce Agentforce | Salesforce | What happens when AI shifts from assistant to autonomous digital labor? |
| 04 | Executive Playbook | Cross-case synthesis (01–03) | What decisions matter most and in what sequence? |
| 05 | Adobe, Google, Anthropic Strategy Library | Adobe, Google/Alphabet, Anthropic | How do incumbents and challengers navigate build/buy/partner, monetization, and competitive dynamics? |
| 06 | Case-by-Case Deep Dives | Adobe, Google, Anthropic | What were the specific decisions, tradeoffs, and outcomes at each company? |
| 07 | Strategy Quick Reference Cards | All cases | Decision frameworks, financial benchmarks, and checklists for rapid executive use |

The goal is not mechanical case summary. It is to extract the practical lessons that matter to leaders navigating AI transitions — where value shifts, what capabilities matter, how governance should evolve, what operating model changes are required, and where companies are most likely to misstep.

## How the cases connect

The six cases form a coherent picture when read together:

**The Value Chain case (01)** provides the structural map. It shows that AI is a multi-layer stack and that competitive advantage can sit at any layer — data, model, fine-tuning, guardrails, API, application, or infrastructure. This is the lens through which every other case should be evaluated.

**Moderna (02)** shows what enterprise-wide AI adoption looks like in practice — platform thinking, democratized access, workforce training, and layered governance inside a regulated environment. It answers the question every large organization faces: how do you go from pilot to scale?

**Salesforce Agentforce (03)** pushes the frontier forward from AI-as-copilot to AI-as-digital-labor. It surfaces the pricing, trust, management, and organizational redesign challenges that emerge when AI performs work autonomously rather than assisting humans.

**Adobe (05/06)** demonstrates how a creative-software incumbent navigated the GenAI threat by building its own model, choosing commercially safe training data, and integrating AI into existing products rather than launching standalone tools. It is the strongest case on stakeholder management, IP strategy, and values-as-competitive-advantage.

**Google / AI Wars (05/06)** illustrates the innovator's dilemma at massive scale — the company that invented generative AI struggling to commercialize it without cannibalizing its core search advertising business. It provides the sharpest data on AI unit economics, the open-vs-closed debate, and the tensions between speed and risk aversion.

**Anthropic (05/06)** presents the strategic crossroads facing a safety-first AI company as the market shifts from LLMs to autonomous agents. It offers lessons on mission-driven talent retention, enterprise market positioning, and how principled constraint can become competitive advantage.

## Master strategic themes across all six cases

### 1. AI advantage is rarely about having the best model

The value-chain case establishes this structurally. Adobe proves it by winning with commercially safe data and workflow integration rather than frontier model scale. Moderna proves it by creating enterprise value through broad access and training, not model ownership. Salesforce proves it by making trust and workflow position the basis for its agent strategy. Google's internal memo warned "we have no moat" despite having invented the Transformer.

**Implication:** stop asking "which model should we use?" and start asking "which layer of the stack fits our assets, economics, and strategic position?"

### 2. Platform thinking beats isolated pilots

Moderna built a shared digital and AI foundation that let the company scale across many use cases. Adobe integrated GenAI as a "superpower" across its product suite rather than launching a standalone tool. Salesforce embedded agents into its existing CRM platform. Google leveraged its cloud, search, and productivity suite as distribution channels.

**Implication:** firms that treat AI as a scattered collection of experiments will struggle. Firms that build shared data, tooling, standards, and reuse mechanisms compound learning and value.

### 3. Trust is a strategic asset, not a compliance checkbox

This is the most consistent theme across all six cases. Salesforce weaponized its trust reputation to sell enterprise agents. Adobe's commercially safe training and Content Credentials turned values into competitive differentiation. Anthropic's safety-first approach won the Department of Defense and attracted mission-driven talent. Google's risk aversion, while slowing its response, reflected genuine concern about AI liability.

**Implication:** trust architecture — security, provenance, auditability, escalation design, human oversight — should be designed as part of market strategy, not bolted on after product launch.

### 4. Democratization creates energy; governance keeps it usable

Moderna gave broad AI access and generated 1,400+ custom GPTs — but also discovered duplication, hallucination risk, and governance gaps. Adobe's cross-functional planning ensured all stakeholders shaped the strategy. Anthropic's Constitutional AI provided scalable, principle-based oversight.

**Implication:** the operating question is not whether to democratize AI, but how to do it with layered governance — light-touch for low-risk productivity, formal review for regulated or consequential decisions.

### 5. AI changes the operating model, not just productivity

Salesforce showed that managers will oversee both human and digital workers. Adobe's adoption required cross-functional alignment across research, product, design, marketing, and strategy. Moderna's AI Academy became a mechanism for organizational change, not just skill development. Google's integration of AI into Search required rethinking its entire advertising and content liability model.

**Implication:** expect organizational redesign, new metrics, new accountability models, and new management capabilities. The question is not only "where can AI help employees?" but "how should work be restructured?"

### 6. No one has solved AI monetization yet

OpenAI has 700M+ weekly active users and projects losses until 2029. Anthropic is burning $3B/year. Google risks cannibalizing its ad model. Perplexity's $60 CPM ads face advertiser skepticism. Adobe is absorbing inference costs into existing subscriptions. Salesforce is experimenting with outcome-based pricing for digital labor.

**Implication:** plan for a multi-year investment period. Model scenarios with declining per-unit costs but rapidly growing usage. Experiment across subscription, usage, bundled, enterprise, and API models.

### 7. The winning change narrative is augmentation, not replacement

Both Moderna and Salesforce show that adoption improves when AI is framed around mission, innovation, quality, and freeing human capacity for higher-value work. Adobe's message since 2016 was that AI would "amplify human intelligence, not replace it." Anthropic's talent stayed because of mission alignment, not just compensation.

**Implication:** pair automation gains with a visible reinvestment story. If the only message is cost reduction, the organization becomes defensive.

## Cross-case strategic framework

### Where to play

Choose the layer where your company can win:

| Layer | Play to strength if you have... |
|-------|-------------------------------|
| Infrastructure / compute | Scale, capital, technical depth (Google TPUs, Nvidia) |
| Foundation models | Research talent, training data, safety expertise (Anthropic, OpenAI) |
| Data / fine-tuning / domain adaptation | Privileged proprietary data or workflows (Adobe Stock, Moderna clinical data) |
| Applications / workflow integration | User context, operating processes, distribution (Adobe Creative Cloud, Salesforce CRM) |
| Trust / governance / compliance | Regulated market presence, security reputation (Salesforce Trust, Adobe Content Credentials) |
| Distribution / ecosystem | Developer community, marketplace, platform position (Google, Meta open source) |

### How to win

The cases converge on a repeatable pattern:

1. Build the digital foundation first (Moderna)
2. Identify a platform logic, not isolated tools (all cases)
3. Choose build/buy/partner based on how core AI is to your value proposition (Adobe = build; Anthropic = partner for distribution; Google = hybrid)
4. Democratize experimentation with layered governance (Moderna, Adobe)
5. Use internal deployment as a proving ground (Salesforce "Customer Zero," Adobe design team)
6. Redesign workflows, management systems, and metrics (Salesforce, Moderna)
7. Shift pricing and business models as AI moves from feature to labor substitute (Salesforce, Google)
8. Communicate augmentation + capacity redeployment, not just automation (all cases)

### What to avoid

- Treating AI as a side initiative without operating model change
- Equating AI strategy with "which model do we use"
- Scaling broken processes instead of redesigning them
- Confusing broad access with sufficient governance
- Assuming trust can be bolted on after launch
- Over-indexing on model benchmarks instead of workflow economics
- Measuring tool usage instead of business outcomes
- Waiting too long to address pricing and business model change
- Letting IT own everything (business leaders should own transformed outcomes)
- Using fear as the primary change lever
