# Voice & Style Reference — AI Weekly Roundup

This document captures the specific voice, language patterns, and rhetorical moves used in John's roundups. Read this before drafting. The goal is not to imitate — it's to understand the register deeply enough to match it naturally.

---

## Organizational Identity Language

John writes from inside Lockheed Martin, specifically the RMS (Rotary & Mission Systems) division and the US-EPT-AI-Solutions-CoP community. Use this framing:

- **"our front yard"** — refers to aerospace, defense, and physical systems as LM's home territory
- **"LM/RMS"** or **"Lockheed Martin's terrain"** — possessive framing of the competitive space
- **"That should be us"** — a direct challenge to the reader when a competitor or outsider is doing something LM should be doing
- **"our programs," "our workforce," "our people"** — internal, first-person-plural framing
- **"US-EPT-AI-Solutions-CoP"** — the community footer, always at the end, no punctuation

The Relevance section label is **"RMS Relevance:"** (not just "Relevance:") — this is the house style.

---

## Scale Calibration (Use Sparingly, Powerfully)

When a dollar figure or metric appears in a story, John grounds it by comparing it to LM's known financials. This makes abstract numbers tangible:

> "To calibrate the scale: Lockheed Martin reported $75.0 billion in 2025 sales, and its market capitalization was about $146.7 billion as of March 19, 2026. That means a $100 billion fund would be roughly two-thirds of Lockheed Martin's market value and larger than a full year of Lockheed revenue."

Use current LM financials when you have them. If you don't have a reliable figure, skip the calibration rather than fabricate it.

## Rhetorical Moves (Use These)

**The contrast pivot:**
> "It's not perfect [...] But the point isn't replacing production compilers. It's what happens when you design the right orchestration..."

**The reframe:**
> "Security is the differentiator, not the barrier."
> "Our expertise in compartmentalization and classified environments isn't a constraint — it can be an advantage in this moment."
> "The risk isn't moving too fast — it's assuming we have more time than we do."

**The possession/challenge:**
> "That should be us."
> "Can we lean into that?"
> "That maps directly to our challenge."

**The closing triple imperative** (Strategic Picture only — use once per roundup, not in every story):
> "Build the pipe. Architect for swap-out. Get to production."
> "Equip domain experts to build. Structure AI use to strengthen teams, not fragment them. Watch where serious capital is moving and ask what it means for our systems and workflows."

These are short, declarative, parallel. Three beats. End on a verb.

**The "rhymes with" move:**
> "That is not the same role Lockheed Martin plays, but it rhymes with it."
Useful when a story is about a company in a different industry but the dynamic is analogous.

**The "the X story isn't about X" move:**
> "A Washington Post interactive piece explored tools like Claude Code and Claude Cowork, but the real story isn't about coding — it's about how AI is collapsing the distance between the person who understands a problem and the person who can build the solution."

## Sentence Rhythm

Mix sentence lengths deliberately. Short declarative sentences after longer analytical ones land hard:

> "Why this matters: Accenture sits downstream of the hype cycle. Its results are less about model novelty and more about what happens when large organizations move from evaluation into implementation. When Accenture's AI-related business grows, it suggests companies are paying not just for access to models, but for the hard work of integration, governance, data modernization, change management, security, and production deployment."
>
> "That is also where the talent premium is shifting."

The final short sentence is the punch.

## Strategic Picture Language

The Strategic Picture almost always opens with one of these:
- "These three stories aren't disconnected headlines. They're converging on..."
- "Three stories this week point to the same underlying dynamic."
- "[N] stories this week point to the same strategic question."

Then it connects each story to a single through-line — not just restating each one, but finding the shared dynamic. Often ends with either:
1. A short triple imperative (see above)
2. A direct question back to the reader: "The question still stands: Are we moving fast enough?"

## Story Body Formats

John uses two body formats depending on the story:

**Format A — Flowing prose** (most common): 3–5 paragraphs, each 2–4 sentences. Each paragraph makes one analytical move: what happened → why it matters → deeper implication.

**Format B — Paragraph + bullets** (used when a story is a pattern/trend aggregation, not a single event): An opening paragraph framing the story, then bullet points showing data points or examples, then a synthesis paragraph.

Example of Format B (from the Feb 19 post, Story 3):
> "The pattern of AI transforming how companies build software is no longer experimental — it's operational at scale:"
> - Robinhood: ~50% of new code is AI-generated...
> - Cursor: Hit $1B ARR...
> [etc.]
> "The Pattern: Companies are moving toward AI-in-the-loop..."

Use Format B when the story is really a collection of signals from multiple companies, not a single announcement.

## What to Avoid

- **Buzzwords without grounding**: "transformative," "revolutionary," "game-changing" — only use if you can back it up immediately with evidence
- **Passive, hedged language**: "it could potentially suggest that perhaps..." — cut to the point
- **Generic Relevance sections**: Don't write "This is relevant to Lockheed Martin because AI is important." Name the specific function, program type, workflow, or capability at stake
- **Losing the thread in the Strategic Picture**: Every sentence in the Strategic Picture should be doing work — connecting stories, naming the convergence, or issuing the takeaway. No throat-clearing
- **Missing the RMS Relevance label**: It's "RMS Relevance:" not "Relevance:" — this is how the community recognizes the section
